# Interpreting Value Snapshot Metrics

Quick reference guide for understanding what the numbers mean and what to look for.

## Operating Margin Benchmarks

### Excellent (>20%)
- **Examples:** Software, platforms, luxury brands
- **Why:** High pricing power, low variable costs, strong moat
- **Watch for:** Sustainability - can margins hold or expand?

### Good (12-20%)
- **Examples:** Branded consumer goods, specialized industrials
- **Why:** Solid differentiation, decent pricing power
- **Watch for:** Competitive dynamics, margin trends

### Modest (5-12%)
- **Examples:** Retailers, commoditized manufacturing
- **Why:** Competitive markets, limited differentiation
- **Watch for:** Cost discipline, volume growth needed

### Poor (<5%)
- **Examples:** Grocers, commodity businesses, distressed companies
- **Why:** Intense competition, no pricing power
- **Watch for:** Scale advantages, niche opportunities

---

## ROIC Benchmarks

### Outstanding (>30%)
- **Signal:** Strong competitive moat, exceptional efficiency
- **Examples:** Asset-light platforms, dominant brands, network effects
- **Implication:** High-quality business worth premium valuation
- **Question:** Is this sustainable or temporary?

### Strong (20-30%)
- **Signal:** Solid competitive position, good capital efficiency
- **Examples:** Quality franchises, well-run operations
- **Implication:** Attractive business if reasonably valued
- **Question:** What drives the returns?

### Adequate (10-20%)
- **Signal:** Decent business, acceptable returns
- **Examples:** Mature industries, stable operations
- **Implication:** Requires attractive valuation for investment
- **Question:** Any catalysts for improvement?

### Weak (<10%)
- **Signal:** Commodity economics or poor execution
- **Examples:** Capital-intensive industries, struggling businesses
- **Implication:** Generally avoid unless special situation
- **Question:** Is there a turnaround story?

### Negative
- **Signal:** Destroying value, operating losses
- **Implication:** High risk - avoid unless deep restructuring/turnaround case
- **Question:** Path to profitability?

## Asset Profile Analysis

### Asset-Light Business
**Characteristics:**
- Net PP&E < 20% of revenue
- High cash generation
- Working capital dominant

**Examples:**
- Software/SaaS (Microsoft, Salesforce)
- Platforms (PDD, Meta, Google)
- Asset-light services

**Implications:**
- Can scale without proportional capital investment
- High ROIC potential (often >30%)
- Capital efficiency = competitive advantage
- Look for: Network effects, switching costs, strong brands

**Risk:** Technology disruption, competition

---

### Moderate Asset Base
**Characteristics:**
- Net PP&E 20-50% of revenue
- Balanced capital needs
- Mix of tangible and intangible value

**Examples:**
- Branded retail (Timberland)
- Restaurants
- Specialized manufacturing

**Implications:**
- Need some capital for growth
- ROIC typically 15-25%
- Brand value important
- Look for: Pricing power, efficient store/factory footprint

**Risk:** Capital intensity limits flexibility

---

### Capital-Intensive Business
**Characteristics:**
- Net PP&E > 50% of revenue
- Heavy fixed asset requirements
- Significant capex to maintain/grow

**Examples:**
- Manufacturing (autos, steel)
- Utilities
- Telecom infrastructure
- Airlines

**Implications:**
- High capital requirements limit returns
- ROIC typically 5-15%
- Scale matters more
- Look for: Efficiency gains, pricing discipline, regulated returns

**Risk:** Technology obsolescence, cyclicality

---

## Cash Position Analysis

### Fortress Balance Sheet
**Signal:** Cash > 50% of market cap
**Examples:** PDD ($58.5B cash, $151B market cap = 39%)
**Implications:**
- Strong financial flexibility
- Question: Why not returning to shareholders or reinvesting?
- Potential: Acquisitions, buybacks, or market signal of uncertainty
- Watch for: Capital allocation decisions

### Healthy Cash
**Signal:** Cash = 10-25% of revenue
**Implications:**
- Adequate operating buffer
- Good working capital management
- Financial stability

### Cash-Tight
**Signal:** Cash < 5% of revenue
**Implications:**
- Limited flexibility
- May need financing for growth
- Higher risk in downturns
- **Red flag if:** Also low/negative working capital

### Cash-Poor
**Signal:** Negative working capital (current liabilities > current assets)
**Implications:**
- **Red flag:** Liquidity concerns
- May indicate business model issue or distress
- Exception: Some retailers operate with negative WC successfully (fast inventory turn)

## Red Flags to Watch

### 1. Negative ROIC
**What it means:** Destroying value
**Action:** Avoid unless turnaround story with clear path

### 2. Declining Operating Margins
**What it means:** Losing pricing power or cost control
**Action:** Investigate competitive dynamics

### 3. High Goodwill (>50% of book value)
**What it means:** Built through acquisitions, not organic
**Action:** Discount tangible book value, risk of impairments

### 4. Negative Working Capital + Low Cash
**What it means:** Potential liquidity crisis
**Action:** High risk - be very careful

### 5. Low ROIC (<10%) + High Valuation
**What it means:** Market optimism not justified by fundamentals
**Action:** Avoid - poor risk/reward

---

## Green Flags to Look For

### 1. High ROIC (>25%) + Growing
**What it means:** Strengthening competitive position
**Action:** Strong candidate if reasonably valued

### 2. Asset-Light + High Margins
**What it means:** Scalable, capital-efficient model
**Action:** Quality business - focus on valuation

### 3. Improving Margins + Steady Revenue
**What it means:** Operational improvements paying off
**Action:** Potential value creation story

### 4. Fortress Balance Sheet + High ROIC
**What it means:** Quality + safety
**Action:** Attractive in uncertain times

### 5. Low Valuation (Market Cap < 3× Operating Earnings) + Solid ROIC (>15%)
**What it means:** Potentially undervalued
**Action:** Deep dive - why is market skeptical?

## Comparative Analysis Framework

### Step 1: Assess Business Quality
- Operating margin level
- ROIC level and trend
- Asset intensity
- Cash generation

### Step 2: Identify Business Type
- Asset-light platform vs capital-intensive
- Mature vs growth
- Cyclical vs stable

### Step 3: Evaluate Valuation
- Market cap vs operating earnings (rough P/E before financial items)
- Enterprise value vs operating earnings
- Compare to peers and history

### Step 4: Look for Catalysts or Concerns
- Margin expansion opportunities
- Capital efficiency improvements
- Market misunderstanding or temporary issues
- Or: Competitive threats, margin pressure, capital allocation concerns

### Step 5: Make Investment Decision
- Quality score (margins, ROIC, asset profile)
- Valuation score (price vs earning power)
- Risk assessment (red flags vs green flags)
- Expected return vs alternatives

## Quick Decision Heuristics

### Strong Buy Candidates
- ROIC >25% + Operating Margin >15% + Reasonable valuation
- Growing margins + Improving ROIC + Low price
- Asset-light + High cash generation + Market cap <5× operating earnings

### Investigate Further
- ROIC 15-25% + Solid margins + Fair valuation
- Improving trends + Turnaround story + Adequate balance sheet

### Probably Pass
- ROIC <10% + Low margins + No clear catalyst
- High goodwill + Declining margins + High valuation
- Negative working capital + Low cash + Weak earnings

### Definite Pass
- Negative ROIC + Losses + Liquidity concerns
- Declining margins + Declining ROIC + High debt
- Low-quality business + High valuation + No moat
